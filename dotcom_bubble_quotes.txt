DOT-COM BUBBLE: VERIFIED BULLISH/EUPHORIC QUOTES AND CALLS (1997-2000)
========================================================================

Compiled from web research. All attributions are real and verifiable.


1. HENRY BLODGET (Merrill Lynch / CIBC Oppenheimer)
----------------------------------------------------
- December 16, 1998: As a CIBC Oppenheimer analyst, Blodget raised his
  Amazon price target from $150 to $400 when it was trading at $240.
  Amazon hit $400 within three weeks. The stock later reached ~$600 in
  late 1999 before crashing 90%+.

- Blodget later said: "I expect my obituary will read, 'Henry Blodget,
  he predicted Amazon would hit $400.'"

- Internal emails (revealed by AG Eliot Spitzer in 2002): While publicly
  issuing top "buy" ratings, Blodget privately called stocks "junk,"
  "crap," and "a powder keg." He called Excite "such a piece of crap!"
  while Merrill gave it a near-top rating. He described InfoSpace as
  "junk" while giving it the highest buy rating (1-1).

- SEC outcome: In 2003, permanently barred from the securities industry,
  paid $4 million ($2M penalties + $2M disgorgement).

Sources:
  - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Blodget
  - https://www.sec.gov/litigation/complaints/comp18115b.htm
  - https://www.sec.gov/news/press/2003-56.htm
  - https://content.time.com/time/specials/packages/article/0,28804,1907771_1907778_1907820,00.html


2. MARY MEEKER (Morgan Stanley) - "Queen of the Net"
-----------------------------------------------------
- Dubbed "Queen of the Net" by Barron's magazine. The single most
  influential voice in internet stock research during the bubble.

- Her annual Internet Report (first published 1995) was required reading
  on Wall Street.

- By 1999, Meeker freely acknowledged valuations were getting "crazy" and
  compared the situation to the Dutch tulip mania -- yet she continued
  issuing "buy" ratings on Amazon, eBay, and Yahoo.

- Throughout the post-2000 downturn, she continued pressing the "buy"
  button on Amazon, eBay, and Yahoo, and kept promoting the rise in
  internet advertising and broadband.

- In early 2001, when broadband was at only 10% of internet users, she
  predicted it would reach 40% by 2005 (this one actually proved roughly
  correct).

- Questioned in fraud investigations after the crash but was not charged
  with wrongdoing.

Sources:
  - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Meeker
  - https://www.thestreet.com/opinion/mary-meeker-a-love-letter-to-net-queen-10247168


3. JAMES GLASSMAN & KEVIN HASSETT - "Dow 36,000" (Published Sept 1999)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
- Core thesis: Stocks in 1999 were significantly UNDERVALUED. The Dow
  would reach 36,000 by 2002 or 2004. They argued stocks had no more
  risk than bonds in the long run.

- From the book: "If you are worried about missing the market's big move
  upward, you will discover that it is not too late. Stocks are now in
  the midst of a one-time-only rise to much higher ground -- to the
  neighborhood of 36,000 on the Dow Jones industrial average."

- Glassman stated: "We say in the book that the Dow should be at 36,000
  today, but that realistically the process should take about five years."

- January 2000 issue of The Atlantic: They bet a critic: "If the Dow is
  closer to 10,000 than to 36,000 ten years from now [i.e., below
  23,000], we will each give $1,000 to the charity of your choice."

- In early 2010, both conceded they lost the bet and donated $1,000 each
  to the Salvation Army.

- Described by Fortune magazine as "the most spectacularly wrong
  investing book ever."

- The Dow didn't actually reach 36,000 until November 2021 -- 22 years
  late.

Sources:
  - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dow_36,000
  - https://fortune.com/2021/11/01/dow-36000-jones-industrial-average-investing-book-record-high-djia-james-glassman-kevin-hassett/
  - https://www.cnn.com/2021/11/03/investing/premarket-stocks-trading


4. CHARLES KADLEC (Seligman Advisors) - "Dow 100,000" (Published 1999)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------
- Book: "Dow 100,000: Fact or Fiction" (1999)

- Predicted the DJIA would reach 100,000 by 2020, citing: the end of the
  Cold War, aging baby boomers, the technological revolution, the spread
  of freedom, and increased competition among governments.

- Kadlec argued this was "only an 11.1 percent annual increase" and was
  entirely realistic.

- (The Dow was around 44,000 at end of 2024 -- less than half his target.)

Sources:
  - https://www.amazon.com/Dow-100-000-Fact-Fiction/dp/0735201374


5. ABBY JOSEPH COHEN (Goldman Sachs)
-------------------------------------
- Goldman Sachs' chief investment strategist. Named Institutional
  Investor's top strategist in 1998 and 1999.

- October 2000 (market already declining): Stuck with her forecast that
  the S&P 500 would end 2000 at 1,575 and reach 1,650 within 12 months.
  The S&P actually ended 2000 at 1,320, was at 1,091 a year later, and
  bottomed at 776 in 2002.

- To her credit: In March 2000, at the peak, she recommended reducing
  stock exposure including tech -- her first such call since January 1999.
  But she remained broadly bullish throughout the decline.

Sources:
  - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abby_Joseph_Cohen
  - https://www.cxoadvisory.com/individual-gurus/abby-joseph-cohen/


6. JACK GRUBMAN (Salomon Smith Barney) - Telecom Analyst
---------------------------------------------------------
- Wall Street's highest-paid telecom analyst during the bubble.

- Had buy recommendations on approximately 30 telecom companies during
  the height of the mania in 1999-2000.

- Maintained "buy" ratings on WorldCom and Global Crossing long after
  fundamentals deteriorated. Only downgraded WorldCom to "neutral" after
  it had already lost 90% of its value.

- Boasted to a rival banker: "When it comes to [WorldCom CEO] Bernie
  [Ebbers] and me, there's no Chinese wall."

- Sat in on meetings of the WorldCom board of directors -- while
  supposedly providing independent research.

Sources:
  - https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/pages/frontline/shows/wallstreet/wcom/grubman.html
  - https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/business/2002/07/05/rating-jack-grubman/


7. ED YARDENI (Deutsche Bank Securities)
-----------------------------------------
- One of the early proponents of the "New Economy" thesis and its bullish
  consequences for the stock market.

- In the late 1990s, predicted the S&P 500 could reach 5,000 by 2000.
  The S&P peaked around 1,527 in March 2000.

Sources:
  - https://realinvestmentadvice.com/resources/blog/yardeni-and-the-long-history-of-prediction-problems/


8. LARRY KUDLOW (CNBC commentator)
-----------------------------------
- Persistently bullish commentator throughout the late 1990s and 2000s.

- While specific 1999-era quotes are harder to pin down, his pattern of
  extreme bullishness was consistent. In December 2007, just as the Great
  Recession began, he wrote: "The Bush boom is alive and well. It's
  finishing up its sixth consecutive year with more to come. Yes, it's
  still the greatest story never told."

- His signature phrase "the greatest story never told" was used
  repeatedly across multiple market cycles to dismiss bearish concerns.

Sources:
  - https://archive.thinkprogress.org/larry-kudlow-bad-economic-predictions-f02ebf47c918/
  - https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Larry_Kudlow


9. WIRED MAGAZINE - "The Long Boom" (July 1997)
------------------------------------------------
- Cover declaration: "We're facing 25 years of prosperity, freedom and a
  better environment for the whole world."

- Written by features editor Peter Leyden and futurist Peter Schwartz.

- Projected sustained economic growth and global integration from the
  1980s through 2020.

- Later expanded into a 2000 book. Became a symbol of 1990s techno-
  utopian hubris.

- The New Statesman later called it "a smug neoliberal fantasy."

Sources:
  - https://thenextwavefutures.wordpress.com/2023/08/06/the-long-boom-that-wasnt-wired-schwartz-leyden/
  - https://www.newstatesman.com/ideas/2022/07/end-long-boom-neoliberal-fantasy


10. BARRON'S "BURNING UP" COVER (March 20, 2000) -- THE CONTRARIAN CALL
------------------------------------------------------------------------
- NOTE: This is actually a BEARISH call that proved spectacularly right.
  Included for context.

- Cover featured sacks of money engulfed in flames with "Burning Fast"
  in bold orange type.

- Article by Jack Willoughby: "When will the Internet Bubble burst?" and
  warned "For scores of 'Net upstarts, that unpleasant popping sound is
  likely to be heard before the end of this year."

- Published just 10 days after the Nasdaq hit its all-time peak on
  March 10, 2000.

- Companies named as burning cash -- Netzee, CDnow, Boo, Beenz, eToys,
  Flooz, Kozmo, Netivation -- none survived.

Sources:
  - https://www.benzinga.com/general/education/19/03/11389594/this-day-in-market-history-barrons-calls-dot-com-bubble-top
  - https://finance.yahoo.com/news/day-market-history-barrons-calls-144641735.html


11. ALAN GREENSPAN - "IRRATIONAL EXUBERANCE" (Dec 5, 1996) -- WARNING
----------------------------------------------------------------------
- NOTE: Another contrarian/warning call, included for context. This was
  3+ years BEFORE the peak.

- "But how do we know when irrational exuberance has unduly escalated
  asset values, which then become subject to unexpected and prolonged
  contractions?"

- The market initially dipped on this speech but then doubled+ before
  the actual crash came. His warning was years too early.

- Robert Shiller (Yale) used the phrase as the title of his book
  "Irrational Exuberance" published March 2000 -- the month of the peak.

Sources:
  - https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_exuberance
  - https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/pages/frontline/shows/dotcon/historical/shiller.html


SUMMARY OF KEY EUPHORIC THEMES (1997-2000):
============================================
- "Stocks are undervalued" (Glassman/Hassett -- at the peak)
- "New economy" / "new paradigm" (Yardeni, many others)
- "25 years of prosperity" (Wired)
- Earnings don't matter, "people pay for concepts" (common refrain)
- Dow 36,000 / Dow 100,000 predictions
- Analysts privately calling stocks "junk" while publicly rating "buy"
- Conflicts of interest: analysts sitting on boards of companies they rated
