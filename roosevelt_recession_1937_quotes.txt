PESSIMISTIC / BEARISH QUOTES FROM THE 1937-1938 RECESSION
("The Roosevelt Recession" / "Depression Within a Depression")
=============================================================
Market fell ~60% from Feb 1937 peak. S&P fell ~55%, Dow fell ~49%.
Industrial production fell 32-40%. Unemployment surged from 14.3% to ~20%.

All quotes are real, sourced, and verifiable.


1. HENRY MORGENTHAU JR. (Treasury Secretary) -- May 9, 1939
   Before the House Ways and Means Committee (recorded in Morgenthau's diary):

   "We have tried spending money. We are spending more than we have ever
   spent before and it does not work. ... I say after eight years of this
   Administration we have just as much unemployment as when we started.
   And an enormous debt to boot!"

   Source: Morgenthau Diaries, Franklin Roosevelt Presidential Library,
   Hyde Park, NY, microfilm roll #50, frame #41. Published in John Morton
   Blum, "From the Morgenthau Diaries: Years of Urgency, 1938-1941" (1965).


2. REP. MAURY MAVERICK (D-TX) -- 1938
   Speaking to fellow House Democrats:

   "Now we Democrats have to admit that we are floundering. We have pulled
   all the rabbits out of the hat, and there are no more rabbits. ... We
   are a confused, bewildered group of people, and we are not delivering
   the goods."

   Source: Cited in Lawrence W. Reed, "Great Myths of the Great Depression,"
   Mackinac Center for Public Policy (1998); also in Burton Folsom,
   "New Deal or Raw Deal?" (2008).


3. NEW YORK HERALD TRIBUNE EDITORIAL -- Late 1937
   After Roosevelt took a nine-day fishing trip while the steel market
   plummeted from 83% of capacity to 35%:

   The editors implored Roosevelt to get back to work and called for a
   reversal of the Roosevelt policy "of bitterness and hate, of setting
   class against class and punishing all who disagreed with him."

   Source: Cited in Lawrence W. Reed, "Great Myths of the Great Depression,"
   Mackinac Center for Public Policy (1998).


4. WALTER LIPPMANN (Columnist) -- March 1938
   In his syndicated newspaper column:

   "With almost no important exception every measure he [Roosevelt] has
   been interested in for the past five months has been to reduce or
   discourage the production of wealth."

   Source: Cited in Lawrence W. Reed, "Great Myths of the Great Depression,"
   Mackinac Center for Public Policy (1998).


5. FRANKLIN D. ROOSEVELT -- April 14, 1938
   Fireside Chat #12, "On the Recession":

   "Five years ago we faced a very serious problem of economic and social
   recovery. For four and a half years that recovery proceeded apace. It is
   only in the past seven months that it has received a visible setback."

   "It is only within the past two months, as we have waited patiently to
   see whether the forces of business itself would counteract it, that it
   has become apparent that government itself can no longer safely fail to
   take aggressive government steps to meet it."

   "Every day that a workman is unemployed, or a machine is unused, or a
   business organization is marking time, is a loss to the Nation. Because
   of idle men and idle machines this Nation lost one hundred billion dollars
   between 1929 and the spring of 1933."

   "It is going to cost something to get out of this recession this way but
   the profit of getting out of it will pay for the cost several times over."

   Source: Miller Center, University of Virginia; American Presidency Project,
   UC Santa Barbara; FDR Presidential Library.


6. MARRINER ECCLES (Federal Reserve Chairman) -- 1939
   Reflecting on the causes of the 1937-38 recession:

   "[The] too rapid withdrawal of the government's stimulus... accompanied
   by other important factors... [led to the] rapid deflation in the fall
   of 1937, which continued until the present spending program of the
   government was begun last summer."

   Source: Federal Reserve History (federalreservehistory.org),
   "Recession of 1937-38."


7. MARRINER ECCLES (Federal Reserve Chairman) -- February 17, 1937
   Quoted in the Wall Street Journal, warning of inflation risk just before
   the Fed's fateful tightening:

   "The short-term rates are excessively low and there may be a tendency
   for rates near the vanishing point to increase."

   Source: Wall Street Journal, February 17, 1937; cited in Federal Reserve
   History essay on the Recession of 1937-38.


8. HAROLD ICKES (Secretary of the Interior) -- December 1937
   On what he called a "capital strike" by big business:

   "The United States is to have its first general sit-down strike -- not
   of labor, not of the American people -- but of the Sixty Families and
   of the capital created by the whole American people of which the Sixty
   Families have obtained control."

   He warned that left unchecked, they would create "big-business Fascist
   America -- an enslaved America."

   Source: Harold Ickes, "The Secret Diary of Harold L. Ickes" (1954);
   also cited in Wikipedia, "Recession of 1937-1938."


9. WALTER LIPPMANN (on FDR, earlier assessment still circulating in the period)
   Lippmann's famous characterization of Roosevelt:

   "A pleasant man who, without any important qualifications for the office,
   would very much like to be President."

   Source: Originally from 1932; widely recirculated during the recession.
   Cited in Ronald Steel, "Walter Lippmann and the American Century" (1980).


=============================================================
CONTEXT AND SEVERITY OF THE 1937-38 CRASH
=============================================================

-- The stock market fell approximately 60% from its March 1937 peak to
   its March 1938 trough (some measures show 49-58% depending on index).

-- In just six months (August 1937 to January 1938), the economy dropped
   as sharply as it had during the thirteen months following the 1929 crash.
   (Source: Federal Reserve History)

-- Stock prices fell 58%, employment dropped 28%, payrolls and industrial
   production declined 43%. (Source: Federal Reserve History)

-- The steel market plummeted from 83% of capacity to 35% in barely two
   months during late 1937. (Source: Reed, "Great Myths of the Great
   Depression")

-- Unemployment jumped from 14.3% in May 1937 to 19.0% in June 1938.
   (Source: Wikipedia, "Recession of 1937-1938")

-- The market value of stocks traded on the NYSE fell by more than 40%.
   (Source: FEE.org, "America's Depression within a Depression, 1937-39")

-- Causes included: the Fed doubling reserve requirements between August
   1936 and May 1937; Roosevelt slashing government spending in 1937;
   and the political hostility toward business ("economic royalists").
   (Source: Federal Reserve History; Digital History, University of Houston)


=============================================================
KEY TERMS USED AT THE TIME
=============================================================

-- "Roosevelt Recession" -- coined by Republicans to pin blame on FDR
-- "Depression within a Depression" -- used by economists and press
-- "Capital strike" -- used by New Dealers like Harold Ickes to blame
   business for refusing to invest
-- "Economic royalists" -- FDR's own term for wealthy opponents
-- "The Sixty Families" -- populist reference to America's wealthiest,
   from Ferdinand Lundberg's 1937 book "America's 60 Families"
