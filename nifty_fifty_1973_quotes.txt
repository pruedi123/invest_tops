BULLISH / EUPHORIC QUOTES FROM THE 1972-1973 MARKET PEAK
=========================================================
S&P 500 peaked January 11, 1973 (Dow hit record 1,067)
The "Nifty Fifty" one-decision stocks at peak valuations
1973-74 bear market: S&P 500 fell ~45%, many Nifty Fifty stocks fell 70-90%


1. BARRON'S ROUNDTABLE HEADLINE (January 1973)
-----------------------------------------------
"Not a Bear Among Them"
-- Barron's annual roundtable headline, January 1973
Context: Survey of top money managers and brokerage firm analysts. Every single
participant was bullish on the stock market for 1973. The Dow had just hit a
new record of 1,067 on January 11, 1973.
Source: Barron's Magazine, January 1973 roundtable issue.
Cited by: optionstrategist.com, stray-reflections.com, nasdaq.com, smeadcap.com


2. L.O. HOOPER, W.E. HUTTON & CO. (circa January 1973)
-------------------------------------------------------
"The economy is becoming less and less vulnerable to major booms and busts."
-- L.O. Hooper, W.E. Hutton & Co.
Context: Statement made around the time of the Barron's roundtable, reflecting
the widespread belief that the business cycle had been tamed.
Source: Cited in Stray Reflections, "Magnificently Risky"


3. TIME MAGAZINE (January 1973)
-------------------------------
"1973 is shaping up as a gilt-edged year."
-- Time magazine, January 1973
Additional: "Most Wall Street analysts are convinced that the market will
continue to climb smartly in 1973."
Context: Published just days before the market peaked. The Dow would go on to
lose over 45% of its value over the next 23 months.
Source: Wikipedia "1973-1974 stock market crash" article, citing Time magazine.


4. MYRON SIMONS, WOODEN & CO. (1973)
-------------------------------------
"It's time to turn bullish."
-- Myron Simons, Director of Research, Wooden & Co. brokerage
Context: Published in Forbes magazine during 1973. Simons argued that since
inflation had likely peaked, stocks were attractive.
Source: Cited in Stray Reflections, "Magnificently Risky"; Forbes


5. TREASURY SECRETARY GEORGE SHULTZ (1973)
-------------------------------------------
"There are bargains galore."
"I almost wish I weren't in my present position so I could get my hands on
some money and invest it."
-- George Shultz, U.S. Treasury Secretary (1972-1974)
Context: Shultz was perplexed by the market decline and told a congressional
committee that stocks were cheap. The market continued falling.
Source: Cited in Stray Reflections, "Magnificently Risky"


6. THE "ONE-DECISION STOCK" CONCEPT (1972-1973)
------------------------------------------------
The Nifty Fifty were called "one-decision stocks" -- companies so appealing
that their stocks should always be bought and never sold, regardless of price.
Originally identified by Morgan Guaranty Trust as the fastest-growing
companies around. Analysts argued that "their superior quality justified
any valuation."
-- Widely used term among institutional investors, 1970-1973
Context: The average P/E of the Nifty Fifty was 42x vs. 18-19x for the S&P 500.
Individual stocks: Polaroid P/E 91-95x, McDonald's 71-86x, Disney 71-82x,
Avon 52-65x. More than 20% had P/E ratios above 50x by end of 1972.
Source: Wikipedia "Nifty Fifty"; AAII; Bridgeway Capital; thebubblebubble.com


7. FORBES MAGAZINE RETROSPECTIVE (1977)
----------------------------------------
"The delusion was that these companies were so good, it didn't matter what
you paid for them; their inexorable growth would bail you out."
"What held the Nifty Fifty up? The same thing that held up tulip-bulb prices
in long-ago Holland -- popular delusions and the madness of crowds."
"The Nifty Fifty were taken out and shot one by one."
-- Forbes Magazine, 1977 (retrospective)
Context: Looking back at the wreckage. From their highs, Xerox fell 71%,
Avon 86%, Polaroid 91%, Disney 87%, McDonald's 72%, Coca-Cola 69%.
Source: Forbes 1977, widely cited in financial literature


8. CAROL LOOMIS, FORTUNE MAGAZINE (early 1973)
-----------------------------------------------
"The prices and price-earnings ratios of a few dozen institutional favorites
-- known as 'the Vestal Virgins' -- have fallen only moderately."
"The great majority of stocks have sunk to levels that suggest that they have
become virtual pariahs... many to levels of four or five times expected 1973
earnings."
-- Carol Loomis, "How the Terrible Two-Tier Market Came to Wall Street,"
   Fortune magazine, early 1973
Context: Loomis documented the extreme bifurcation of the market, where the
Nifty Fifty traded at nosebleed valuations while most other stocks were
being crushed. Eli Lilly at ~40x earnings, Avon at ~52x earnings.
Source: Fortune magazine; cited in smeadcap.com "1972+1974=2020"


9. CHARLIE MUNGER (retrospective, on the 1972-73 era)
------------------------------------------------------
"This is the biggest financial euphoria episode of my career [75 years]
because of the totality of it."
-- Charlie Munger (likely from Berkshire annual meeting or interview)
Context: Munger's own investment partnership was devastated during 1973-74.
He also described the mechanics: "When the Nifty 50 were the rage, JP Morgan
talked everybody into buying just 50 stocks and they didn't care what the
price was, they just bought those 50 stocks. Of course in due time, their own
buying forced those 50 stocks up to 60 times earnings whereupon it broke and
everything went down by like two-thirds quite fast."
Source: Cited in smeadcap.com; Yahoo Finance


10. BILL GRIMSLEY, INVESTMENT COMPANY OF AMERICA (1973)
-------------------------------------------------------
"There's really no place to hide."
-- Bill Grimsley, Investment Company of America
Context: As the bear market unfolded, even diversified investors found no
shelter from the carnage.
Source: Cited in Stray Reflections, "Magnificently Risky"


ADDITIONAL CONTEXT
==================

- The Dow Jones Industrial Average surpassed 1,000 for the first time on
  November 14, 1972. Nixon had just been reelected, the economy was growing,
  and corporate profits were surging.

- By January 11, 1973, the Dow hit a new record of 1,067.

- The 1973-74 bear market lasted 694 days (Jan 11, 1973 to Dec 6, 1974).
  The Dow lost over 45% of its value. It was the seventh-worst bear market
  in Dow history.

- Nifty Fifty stocks had averaged over 22% annual earnings growth over the
  prior five years, with over 30% growth in 1972 -- the growth was real,
  but the prices paid were not justified.

- The consensus among fund managers was so strong that "even defensive fund
  managers had difficulty justifying themselves when they were not invested
  in these stocks."

- Alan Abelson, Barron's longtime columnist, later wrote of Wall Street
  forecasters: "Never have so many said so much to such little purpose."
